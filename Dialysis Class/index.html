Reading: What Do the Kidneys Do?

The kidneys are two small organs in the lower back. They are about the size of a fist. Each kidney contains about one million tiny filters called nephrons. Blood flows through the nephrons. The nephrons remove extra water and waste from the blood. This waste and water become urine, which leaves the body.

The kidneys also help control blood pressure. They do this by managing how much fluid is in the body and by releasing certain hormones. When the kidneys work well, the body has the right fluid balance, and the blood is clean. When the kidneys do not work well, waste and fluid can build up. This waste build-up is called uremia. Some symptoms of uremia include feeling tired, swollen, or short of breath.

Dialysis is a treatment that helps when the kidneys cannot do their job. A dialysis machine acts like an artificial kidney. It filters the blood and removes extra fluid. Dialysis technicians help patients during this treatment. They must understand kidney function and use clear, simple language to explain the process to patients.

Reading: How the Nephron Works

The nephron is the basic working unit of the kidney. Each kidney contains about one to two million nephrons, and each nephron performs three main jobs: filtration, reabsorption, and secretion.

Filtration happens in the glomerulus, a small ball of blood vessels. Blood enters the glomerulus under pressure, and water, salts, glucose, and waste products pass into Bowman's capsule. This filtered fluid is called filtrate.

Next, the filtrate moves through the tubule. As the filtrate travels, the body reabsorbs what it needs, such as water, glucose, and certain minerals. This process helps maintain fluid balance and keeps important nutrients in the body.

Finally, secretion occurs. Extra waste products and chemicals move from the blood into the tubule. After filtration, reabsorption, and secretion are complete, the remaining fluid becomes urine. The urine then travels to the bladder through the ureters.

Reading: Understanding Kidney Disease

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) happens when the kidneys slowly lose their ability to filter the blood. Many people do not notice symptoms at first. As the disease gets worse, waste and fluid build up in the body. This can cause swelling in the legs, ankles, or face. It can also cause fatigue and shortness of breath.

Doctors use lab tests to check how well the kidneys are working. They look at the level of waste products in the blood, such as creatinine. They also check how much protein is in the urine. These tests help doctors decide when a patient needs dialysis or a kidney transplant.

Dialysis technicians often see patients who feel worried or confused. Patients may not understand why they need treatment or what CKD means. Technicians can support patients by using simple words, repeating key ideas, and checking for understanding. Clear communication helps patients feel safer and more in control.

Section 4: Medical Sanitation and Review

Reading: Sanitation in Dialysis

In dialysis, sanitation is very important. Germs can enter the blood if equipment is not clean. Dialysis technicians must wash their hands, wear gloves, and use clean supplies. They must follow each step of the cleaning process carefully.

Surfaces should be wiped with approved disinfectants. Needles and other sharp items must be thrown away in special containers. Used dialyzers and tubing must be handled safely. Good sanitation protects both patients and staff from infection.
